TYMR VS MEMORY

Memory tracks your time. Tymr substantiates the company's.

Memory (and similar AI time-trackers like Reclaim and Clockwise) helps an individual or small team understand where hours go. Tymr produces the org-level compliance artifact a CFO, tax firm, or auditor needs. Adjacent categories, not competitors.

The honest summary

Memory and Reclaim.ai are excellent personal and team productivity tools. Passive tracking, focus-block scheduling, smart calendar — the kind of thing an engineer or manager uses to understand their own week.

Tymr isn't a productivity tool. It's an accounting and tax substantiation layer. The buyer is the CFO or Controller; the output is the evidence artifact attached to Form 6765 or the monthly CapEx journal entry. An engineer never opens Tymr to "see where their time went" — that would be a sign we built the wrong thing.

When to pick which

Pick Memory if

You want a personal or team productivity tool

  • An individual engineer or manager wants to understand their own time
  • Your team values automatic, low-friction calendar + app tracking
  • You want focus-time scheduling & calendar optimization
  • The finance org isn't asking for R&D credit or CapEx evidence
Pick Tymr if

You need org-level compliance evidence

  • Your CFO or Tax Director is the actual user
  • You claim (or could claim) the US R&D tax credit
  • You capitalize software costs under ASC 350-40 for SOX
  • You need a defensible artifact for an auditor — not a personal dashboard

They're not substitutes

A few design partners use Memory (or similar) alongside Tymr. One is a productivity tool for engineers; the other is the evidence layer for the CFO. They don't conflict, and neither one does the other's job well.
