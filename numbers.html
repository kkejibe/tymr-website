Where the numbers come from

At the federal level we model the Alternative Simplified Credit (ASC) method: 14% of current-year qualifying spend above 50% of the prior-three-year average. For a typical software org with stable or growing R&D, that comes out to roughly 7–10% of qualifying spend as a federal credit. We default to 8% as a balanced middle.

Regional credits are calibrated against published 2024–2025 R&D credit surveys (BDO, EY, Deloitte, KBKG). Each percentage above represents the effective rate as a percentage of total qualifying spend for a typical software company that has been claiming for 3+ years — accounting for incremental vs non-incremental methods, base-period rules, refundability, and statewide caps. Canada's SR&ED is shown as the federal 15% basic rate.

What counts as qualifying spend? Wages for employees performing qualified research, supplies consumed in R&D, 65% of contract research, and certain capitalized software-development costs. Management and back-office time generally doesn't qualify.

Why the substantiation matters more than the math

The math above is the easy part. The hard part — and the reason most companies under-claim — is substantiation: proving the work actually qualified, with contemporaneous evidence tied to specific projects and engineers.

That's what Tymr produces. Every hour in your R&D credit worksheet traces back to a Jira ticket transition, a GitHub pull request, or a calendar block — time-stamped and immutable. When your tax firm prepares the credit or your company faces an audit, the evidence is already on file.

Slide 09 Methodology — how the sorting works Plain English OpenClose

How we got from source events to qualifying hours.

  • Data sources. Event-level ingest from Jira (status transitions, comments, worklogs), GitHub (commits, PRs, reviews), Google & Microsoft Calendar (meeting blocks with engineering attendees).
  • Attribution. Each event attributes time to (a) the engineer who generated it and (b) the project inferred from Jira/GitHub metadata. Overlapping events deduplicated per the "primary activity" rule.
  • Qualifying classification. Four-part test applied per activity bucket: permitted purpose, technological in nature, elimination of uncertainty, process of experimentation. Rules library is auditable; tags are reviewable and overridable.
  • Capitalize vs expense. Application-development stage separated from preliminary project stage and post-implementation stage using commit/PR lifecycle signals.
  • Human review. Controller reviews low-confidence (<0.85) classifications weekly, about thirty minutes. No engineer survey.
Full methodology PDF ships with every report. Two pages. Designed for a tax partner to defend in an audit.
