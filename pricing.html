The math behind the flat fee

A 100-engineer US software company with a fully-loaded dev cost of $180K/engineer spends roughly $18M/year on engineering. If 55% of that qualifies for the US R&D credit — typical for product-shipping software — that's ~$9.9M in QREs. At a conservative 8% federal ASC rate, that's ~$790K federal credit, plus state (California ~$740K, Massachusetts ~$640K).

$10–20K flat for a pilot that puts a source event behind every qualified hour is a rounding error against that credit — and most teams recover more than the flat fee in removed haircut alone.

Still have questions?

How does the risk reversal actually work?

At the end of the 60-day pilot we hand the pack directly to your tax firm. They review the methodology PDF, the workpaper CSV, and a sample of source-event lineage. If they say "no, this wouldn't hold up in an exam," you walk away at $0 — no invoice, no future obligation. We lose the bet, not you.

Do you replace our tax firm or our accounting software?

No. We don't touch Form 6765, we don't share your tax firm's fees, and we don't replace NetSuite / Sage Intacct / QuickBooks. We produce the per-engineer time evidence your preparer already has to collect — so they're not running quarterly engineer surveys.

Why not seat-based pricing now?

Seat math puts the customer on defense during a design-partner pilot. "Is it 50 engineers or 75? Do observers count?" We'd rather spend that energy on making the pack defensible. Seat-based tiers come once the product is standardized.

What happens after the pilot?

Three paths. (1) Roll wider — flat annual engagement across your whole engineering org. (2) Embed into your tax firm's study proposal as a standardized line item. (3) Walk away with your files — no lock-in, no exit fees.
